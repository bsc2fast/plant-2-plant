1.Plant biocommunication & signalling biology

Real science behind "plants speak" and "plants listen to each other" — what we replay through MQTT and TTS as semantic events.

  1. [1] Khait, I. et al. (2023). Sounds emitted by plants under stress are airborne and informative. Cell, 186(7), 1328–1336. doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2023.03.009

    Relevance — First peer-reviewed evidence that drought- and damage-stressed plants emit airborne ultrasonic clicks decodable by ML. Grounds "plants making sounds humans can't hear" as more than metaphor.

    Cite at — Opening motivation; climate-trigger-to-utterance pipeline.

  2. [2] Mancuso, S. & Viola, A. (2015). Brilliant Green: The Surprising History and Science of Plant Intelligence. Island Press. islandpress.org/books/brilliant-green

    Relevance — Accessible synthesis of Mancuso's plant-neurobiology programme. Anchors framing of plants as agents worth talking with.

    Cite at — Personality-from-biology section; ethical framing.

  3. [3] Mancuso, S. (2017). The Revolutionary Genius of Plants. Atria Books. simonandschuster.com

    Relevance — Extends the case for plant cognition, memory and inter-plant signalling. Defends why a plant deserves a persona, not just a UI.

    Cite at — Persona derivation; plant-rights sidebar.

  4. [4] Mancuso, S. (2021). The Nation of Plants. Other Press. otherpress.com

    Relevance — Frames plants as a polity with rights — a provocation that gives plant-2-plant a political/ethical edge beyond cuteness.

    Cite at — Critical reflection / future-work.

  5. [5] Simard, S. W. et al. (1997). Net transfer of carbon between ectomycorrhizal tree species in the field. Nature, 388, 579–582. doi.org/10.1038/41557

    Relevance — The original "wood wide web" paper — empirical evidence of carbon transfer between trees via mycorrhizal networks. Biological precedent we shortcut via MQTT.

    Cite at — Peer-routing section.

  6. [6] Simard, S. W. (2018). Mycorrhizal networks facilitate tree communication, learning, and memory. In Memory and Learning in Plants (pp. 191–213). Springer. doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-75596-0_10

    Relevance — Reviews evidence that mycorrhizal networks carry signals, not just resources — closer to "plants talking" than 1997.

    Cite at — MQTT-as-mycelium analogy.

  7. [7] Karban, R., Yang, L. H. & Edwards, K. F. (2014). Volatile communication between plants that affects herbivory: a meta-analysis. Ecology Letters, 17(1), 44–52. doi.org/10.1111/ele.12205

    Relevance — Quantitative evidence plants warn neighbours via volatile organic compounds. Justifies proximity-bounded peer topics.

    Cite at — "Why broadcast nearby?" justification.

  8. [8] Heil, M. & Karban, R. (2010). Explaining evolution of plant communication by airborne signals. Trends in Ecology & Evolution, 25(3), 137–144. doi.org/10.1016/j.tree.2009.09.010

    Relevance — Theoretical grounding for why plants would evolve airborne signalling at all — addresses the "is this even communication?" objection.

    Cite at — Footnote on first introduction of inter-plant talk.

  9. [9] Wohlleben, P. (2016). The Hidden Life of Trees. Greystone Books. greystonebooks.com

    Relevance — Pop-science but seminal in shifting public discourse toward plant sociality. The cultural backdrop the project taps into.

    Cite at — Public-perception / motivation paragraph.

2.HCI with plants & botanical interfaces

The project lineage: Botanicalls, Botanicus Interacticus, Florence, MIDI Sprout. We extend by giving each plant a persona, peers, and an LLM voice.

  1. [10] Poupyrev, I. et al. (2012). Botanicus Interacticus: interactive plants technology. ACM SIGGRAPH 2012 Emerging Technologies. doi.org/10.1145/2343456.2343464

    Relevance — Canonical "plant as touch interface" — capacitive sensing turns living plants into input devices.

    Cite at — Related work on botanical interfaces; sensing tier.

  2. [11] Hansen, R., Kuznetsov, S., Paulos, E. & Wang, K. (2006–2009). Botanicalls. Project documentation. botanicalls.com

    Relevance — Plants that phone/tweet when thirsty — the foundational artefact for plants speaking on their own behalf via networked tech. Direct ancestor.

    Cite at — Lineage paragraph; "we extend Botanicalls by..."

  3. [12] Kuznetsov, S. & Paulos, E. (2010). UpStream: motivating water conservation with low-cost water flow sensing and persuasive displays. CHI 2010. doi.org/10.1145/1753326.1753649

    Relevance — Adjacent: ambient persuasive sensing for water/care behaviour change.

    Cite at — Care-feedback loop discussion.

  4. [13] Lee, J., Cho, Y., Kim, K. & Lee, W. (2017). Florence: Designing intelligent plant–human interaction. CHI Extended Abstracts. doi.org/10.1145/3027063.3053085

    Relevance — Closest prior work — explicitly explores giving plants conversational agency for human wellbeing. Cite as "we go further by..."

    Cite at — Related work; differentiation paragraph.

  5. [14] Kuribayashi, S., Sakamoto, Y. & Tanaka, H. (2007). I/O plant: a tool kit for designing augmented human-plant interactions. CHI Extended Abstracts. doi.org/10.1145/1240866.1240898

    Relevance — Early toolkit framing for plant-mediated interaction, prefiguring our kit-per-plant model.

    Cite at — Hardware kit section.

  6. [15] Holstius, D., Kembel, J., Hurst, A., Wan, P.-H. & Forlizzi, J. (2004). Infotropism: living and robotic plants as interactive displays. DIS 2004. doi.org/10.1145/1013115.1013145

    Relevance — Plants as ambient displays driven by digital signals — inverse direction (data-to-plant vs. plant-to-voice). Cite for symmetry.

    Cite at — Related work; ambient-output discussion.

  7. [16] Data Garden. PlantWave: Tune in to Nature. Project page. plantwave.com

    Relevance — Commercial descendant of MIDI Sprout — biopotential-to-MIDI sonification. The lineage we deliberately do not take (we use semantic LLM voice instead).

    Cite at — Design rationale: why language, not sonification.

  8. [17] Kim, S., Lee, B. & Bae, S.-H. (2021). Designing the inner life of a plant: a tangible interaction approach. TEI 2021. doi.org/10.1145/3430524.3442457

    Relevance — Recent TEI work giving plants interior states surfaced through tangibles. Nearby precedent for "plant has moods."

    Cite at — Mood-system section.

3.More-than-human & posthuman HCI

Vocabulary for designing with non-humans, not for humans only. Lets us position plant-2-plant as multispecies design rather than gimmick.

  1. [18] Giaccardi, E. & Redström, J. (2020). Technology and more-than-human design. Design Issues, 36(4), 33–44. doi.org/10.1162/desi_a_00612

    Relevance — Programmatic statement of "more-than-human design" as research agenda. The cleanest single reference for the framing.

    Cite at — First mention of "more-than-human."

  2. [19] Coulton, P. & Lindley, J. G. (2019). More-than human centred design: considering other things. The Design Journal, 22(4), 463–481. doi.org/10.1080/14606925.2019.1614320

    Relevance — Practical reframing of design process to include non-human stakeholders. Pairs with Giaccardi & Redström.

    Cite at — Methods section.

  3. [20] Liu, J., Byrne, D. & Devendorf, L. (2018). Design for collaborative survival: an inquiry into human-fungi relationships. CHI 2018. doi.org/10.1145/3173574.3173614

    Relevance — Explicit more-than-human HCI with fungi — methodological sibling of designing-with-plants.

    Cite at — Methodology / multispecies design section.

  4. [21] Smith, N., Bardzell, S. & Bardzell, J. (2017). Designing for cohabitation: naturecultures, hybrids, and decentering the human in design. CHI 2017. doi.org/10.1145/3025453.3025948

    Relevance — Foundational CHI argument for decentering humans. Justifies why the plant — not the human — is the addressed subject.

    Cite at — Theoretical positioning.

  5. [22] Westerlaken, M. (2020). Imagining multispecies worlds. PhD thesis, Malmö University. mau.diva-portal.org

    Relevance — Thesis-length treatment of designing with non-humans; gives precise multispecies vocabulary.

    Cite at — Theoretical framing.

  6. [23] Heitlinger, S., Bryan-Kinns, N. & Comber, R. (2019). The right to the sustainable smart city. CHI 2019. doi.org/10.1145/3290605.3300517

    Relevance — Heitlinger's strand on more-than-human urban computing. Grounds the political stakes of computational voice for non-humans.

    Cite at — Critical framing; future-work on urban deployments.

  7. [24] Søndergaard, M. L. J. & Hansen, L. K. (2019). Designing with bias and privilege? Nordes 2019. doi.org/10.21606/nordes.2019.018

    Relevance — Critical-feminist framing of design subjects; helps interrogate whose idea of a "plant personality" the LLM encodes.

    Cite at — Critique sidebar on persona authorship.

4.LLM agents — persona & memory

The architectural backbone of the hive: memory streams, persona stability, the perception–brain–action loop.

  1. [25] Park, J. S. et al. (2023). Generative agents: interactive simulacra of human behavior. UIST 2023. doi.org/10.1145/3586183.3606763 · arXiv:2304.03442

    Relevance — Defines memory-stream + reflection + planning architecture for stable LLM personas over time. Direct architectural reference for our hive's per-plant memory.

    Cite at — Persona/memory architecture section.

  2. [26] Shanahan, M., McDonell, K. & Reynolds, L. (2023). Role play with large language models. Nature, 623, 493–498. doi.org/10.1038/s41586-023-06647-8

    Relevance — Frames LLM personas as role-play, not identity — useful caveat when claiming a plant "has" a personality.

    Cite at — Persona section, honesty footnote.

  3. [27] Zhong, W. et al. (2024). MemoryBank: enhancing large language models with long-term memory. AAAI 2024. doi.org/10.1609/aaai.v38i17.29946 · arXiv:2305.10250

    Relevance — Concrete memory store for LLM agents with forgetting curves — directly mappable to "the plant's memory grows."

    Cite at — Memory subsystem section.

  4. [28] Wang, L. et al. (2024). A survey on large language model based autonomous agents. Frontiers of Computer Science, 18(6), 186345. doi.org/10.1007/s11704-024-40231-1 · arXiv:2308.11432

    Relevance — One-stop citation for "LLM agents are a real research area now."

    Cite at — Background on LLM agents.

  5. [29] Xi, Z. et al. (2025). The rise and potential of large language model based agents: a survey. Science China Information Sciences, 68, 121101. doi.org/10.1007/s11432-024-4222-0 · arXiv:2309.07864

    Relevance — Complementary survey emphasising perception–brain–action loop; matches our STT–LLM–TTS pipeline.

    Cite at — Architecture overview.

5.Anthropomorphism & the CASA paradigm

Why a voice will be heard as personhood almost automatically — and the ethical risks of leaning on that.

  1. [30] Reeves, B. & Nass, C. (1996). The Media Equation. CSLI Publications / Cambridge University Press. cambridge.org

    Relevance — Foundational CASA (Computers Are Social Actors) text. Explains why a voice on a plant reads as personhood.

    Cite at — Anthropomorphism section.

  2. [31] Nass, C. & Moon, Y. (2000). Machines and mindlessness: social responses to computers. Journal of Social Issues, 56(1), 81–103. doi.org/10.1111/0022-4537.00153

    Relevance — Empirical synthesis of CASA effects — minimal cues elicit social attribution.

    Cite at — "Why a voice is enough" claim.

  3. [32] Epley, N., Waytz, A. & Cacioppo, J. T. (2007). On seeing human: a three-factor theory of anthropomorphism. Psychological Review, 114(4), 864–886. doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.864

    Relevance — Canonical psychological theory of when and why people anthropomorphise. Useful for predicting which users engage most.

    Cite at — User-experience prediction; ethics.

  4. [33] Salles, A., Evers, K. & Farisco, M. (2020). Anthropomorphism in AI. AJOB Neuroscience, 11(2), 88–95. doi.org/10.1080/21507740.2020.1740350

    Relevance — Critical view — anthropomorphism in AI as ethically risky, not neutral. The doc needs this counterweight.

    Cite at — Critical reflection.

6.Calm, ambient & peripheral computing

The interaction profile we want: mostly idle, periphery-of-attention, occasionally surfacing to the centre.

  1. [34] Weiser, M. & Brown, J. S. (1996). The coming age of calm technology. Xerox PARC. calmtech.com

    Relevance — Defines calm technology — periphery/centre attention shifts. Motivates "mostly idle, occasionally speaks" interaction profile.

    Cite at — Interaction model.

  2. [35] Weiser, M. (1991). The computer for the 21st century. Scientific American, 265(3), 94–104. doi.org/10.1038/scientificamerican0991-94

    Relevance — The ubicomp manifesto. Pair with Calm Tech for the ambient lineage.

    Cite at — Background on ambient computing.

  3. [36] Pousman, Z. & Stasko, J. (2006). A taxonomy of ambient information systems: four patterns of design. AVI 2006. doi.org/10.1145/1133265.1133277

    Relevance — Taxonomy that lets us position plant-2-plant precisely on information capacity, notification level, fidelity and aesthetic emphasis.

    Cite at — Design-space positioning.

  4. [37] McCullough, M. (2004). Digital Ground: Architecture, Pervasive Computing, and Environmental Knowing. MIT Press. mitpress.mit.edu

    Relevance — Place-based pervasive computing — useful for the "plant lives in a room with other plants" spatial framing.

    Cite at — Spatial/proximity routing rationale.

7.Voice agents in the home

What we know about how Alexa/Google Home actually get used, where conversational agents break, and the always-listening privacy concern.

  1. [38] Porcheron, M., Fischer, J. E., Reeves, S. & Sharples, S. (2018). Voice interfaces in everyday life. CHI 2018. doi.org/10.1145/3173574.3174214

    Relevance — Ethnography of how Alexa/Google Home actually get used — turn-taking is awkward, often instrumental. Sets realistic expectations.

    Cite at — Voice-UX section.

  2. [39] Lopatovska, I. et al. (2019). Talk to me: exploring user interactions with the Amazon Alexa. Journal of Librarianship and Information Science, 51(4), 984–997. doi.org/10.1177/0961000618759414

    Relevance — Empirical use-pattern study of a household voice agent — comparable baseline.

    Cite at — Interaction-pattern expectations.

  3. [40] Lau, J., Zimmerman, B. & Schaub, F. (2018). Alexa, are you listening? Privacy perceptions, concerns and privacy-seeking behaviors with smart speakers. CSCW 2018. doi.org/10.1145/3274371

    Relevance — Defines the "always listening" privacy concern empirically. Mandatory citation for the on-device-VAD design choice.

    Cite at — Privacy / on-device tier rationale.

  4. [41] Clark, L. et al. (2019). What makes a good conversation? Challenges in designing truly conversational agents. CHI 2019. doi.org/10.1145/3290605.3300705

    Relevance — Articulates conversational-breakdown failure modes. Relevant to designing graceful failure for a plant that can't actually understand most of what it hears.

    Cite at — Failure-mode / fallback design.

10.Adjacent & complicating literature

Critiques, counter-arguments, and depth references — included so the doc remains intellectually honest rather than persuasive only.

  1. [51] Taiz, L. et al. (2019). Plants neither possess nor require consciousness. Trends in Plant Science, 24(8), 677–687. doi.org/10.1016/j.tplants.2019.05.008

    Relevance — The major critique of plant-neurobiology claims. Cite for intellectual honesty — the science underpinning "plant personality" is contested.

    Cite at — Honesty footnote near first persona claim.

  2. [52] Hall, M. (2011). Plants as Persons: A Philosophical Botany. SUNY Press. sunypress.edu

    Relevance — Philosophical case for plant moral standing. Strongest counterweight if a reviewer dismisses the project as cute anthropomorphism.

    Cite at — Ethics / plant-rights paragraph.

  3. [53] Marder, M. (2013). Plant-Thinking: A Philosophy of Vegetal Life. Columbia University Press. cup.columbia.edu

    Relevance — Continental-philosophy companion to Mancuso — argues for taking vegetal existence seriously on its own terms.

    Cite at — Framing / ethics.

  4. [54] Haraway, D. J. (2016). Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene. Duke University Press. dukeupress.edu

    Relevance — Reference for multispecies / kin-making thinking that more-than-human HCI draws from.

    Cite at — Framing of "designing with plants as kin."

  5. [55] Tsing, A. L. (2015). The Mushroom at the End of the World. Princeton University Press. press.princeton.edu

    Relevance — Multispecies ethnography that's now standard reading in more-than-human design.

    Cite at — Theoretical framing.

★Future paper / poster / demo titles

Seven distinct angles to pick from when the project is mature enough to write up. Venues match the angle, not the other way round.

  1. DIS short "It's been three days": designing care-deficit speech acts for a houseplant LLM agent. Narrow focus on the moment a plant first complains; analyse user reactions and complaint phrasings across personas.
  2. TEI demo Plant-2-Plant: a kit-per-pot toolkit for embodied LLM personas in domestic ecologies. Hands-on demo of the hardware kit + hive; attendees onboard a new plant in five minutes.
  3. alt.CHI Whose voice is the basil's? Authorship, persona, and the ethics of speaking on a plant's behalf. Critical-reflective piece interrogating who actually authors the persona an LLM gives a plant.
  4. NIME Mycelial MQTT: proximity-routed plant-to-plant utterances as a generative ambient soundscape. Frame inter-plant chatter as long-form generative composition; report on a gallery installation.
  5. C&C Personality from photoperiod: deriving stable LLM personas from species biology. Methods-forward paper on the persona-derivation pipeline (species profile → trait vector → system prompt) and perceived consistency.
  6. CHI LBW Calm conversational agents: speech as a peripheral cue in always-on domestic AI. Position plant-2-plant against the smart-speaker baseline; measure attention and interruption costs.
  7. ISEA A Nation of Plants, Networked: speculative governance among twelve LLM-personified houseplants. Art/theory piece building on Mancuso's Nation of Plants; the plants hold a "council" via MQTT and we publish the transcripts.
